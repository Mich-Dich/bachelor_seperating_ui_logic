**Pros:**
- **Enhanced Modularity Through Separation of Concerns**: The MVP pattern is fundamentally built upon a strict separation of concerns, which directly fosters a modular architecture. By cleanly dividing responsibilities among the Model (data and business logic), View (passive user interface), and Presenter (intermediary logic), each component becomes a discrete and independent unit. This high degree of modularity is a foundational element for extensibility, as it allows for more effective independent task assignment within a growing development team. Because each component is decoupled, it can be modified or replaced without affecting others, thereby reducing the risk of cascading changes and enabling parallel development efforts.
- **Accelerated Onboarding and Targeted Skill Development**: The clear definition of roles within MVP can help mitigate the learning curve for new developers joining a project. The pattern provides a defined blueprint for structuring code, which can shorten the average time a new team member needs to understand the system before making a safe change. Furthermore, MVP's separation facilitates collaboration between developers and designers by allowing individuals to focus on specific areas of expertise. A developer can be assigned to work on Presenter logic without needing to understand the intricacies of the UI implementation in the View, or vice versa. This targeted focus reduces the cognitive load during onboarding and allows new members to become productive contributors more quickly.
- **Improved Testability as an Enabler for Safe Extension**: A cornerstone of MVP is its emphasis on testability, which is a critical, though indirect, factor in supporting extensibility. The pattern's design makes it easier to unit test the components of your application, particularly the Presenter, because the business logic is isolated from the UI framework. This facilitates a robust suite of unit tests that act as a safety net. When a codebase is highly testable, developers can extend or modify the system with greater confidence, knowing that regressions are likely to be caught quickly. This assurance reduces the risk associated with making changes and therefore encourages and enables the ongoing evolution and extension of the application.

**Cons:**
- **Proliferation of Boilerplate and Increased Complexity**: A well-documented drawback of the MVP pattern is that it often necessitates a significant amount of boilerplate code, requiring the creation and maintenance of multiple files (e.g., a View interface, a Presenter class, and a View implementation) for each UI component. This added complexity can counterintuitively slow down development, particularly in the initial stages, and make the codebase harder to navigate. The overhead of managing these additional artifacts can act as a drag on extensibility, as even simple extensions may require changes across several layers of the architecture.
- **Potential for Suboptimal Implementation in Large Teams**: While MVP provides a structure, its practical application can become problematic as a system scales to accommodate larger teams. The pattern's interpretation can be vague, and in complex applications, the relationships between presenters and sub-presenters can become unclear. There is a documented risk that when views are split into smaller components, the communication pathways between them and their respective presenters become convoluted. Furthermore, a common pitfall is the migration of business logic from the Model into the Presenter, which violates the intended separation of concerns and can create bloated, hard-to-maintain "god objects" that are a major obstacle to parallel work and independent component extension.
